Incorporating crisis intervention into emergency services ensures people get the help they truly need.

During the pandemic, there has been a renewed spotlight on problems with our policing system in the United States. National debate has shed light on issues around police brutality, inequitable treatment towards minorities, and lack of training for handling crisis situations. In addition to the growing calls for change, the results of our studies illuminated that people not only need access to police, fire, and medical emergency response, they also want alternative response options to police, particularly for matters of mental or behavioral crises.

Crisis intervention programs are one such alternative response. Crisis intervention programs involve mental health professionals partnering with police agencies to help respond to situations that may involve persons with mental illnesses, experiencing homelessness, suffering drug addiction, or other situations. Model programs such as Cahoots in Portland, STAR in Denver, and the Street Crisis Response Team in San Francisco have shown success in partnering with police agencies to help persons in crisis.

Diverting 911 calls relating to mental or behavioral crises away from police and towards professionals in the mental health field has gained so much traction that beginning in July of this year, 988, a three-digit hotline dedicated to crisis response, is being launched nationwide. 988 will be the number people can call to report a mental or behavioral crisis, whether on behalf of one’s own crisis or a crisis that is being witnessed.

The alignment between this national recognition and user responses to our initial accesSOS app design made it clear to us that we needed to incorporate 988 into the app.

Our design for the accesSOS app ensures that both 911 and 988 are accessible to everyone.
