Semi-structured Qualitative Interviews

In order to incorporate 988 into our app design, we knew we needed to develop an understanding of the needs of users that might use the service from experts already involved in mental illness and police response. Therefore, we interviewed Crisis Intervention Specialists in cities across the country.

Findings

Crisis intervention specialists aim to avoid calling the police as much as possible. They only involve the police in situations where the caller/person in question has a weapon, is violent or is actively trying to harm themselves or others.

Crisis intervention specialists try to build trust and create a safe environment for the caller. The majority of calls that crisis intervention specialsits received were those regarding domestic abuse, depression, mental breakdowns, suicide ideation, and anxiety as a result of COVID’s stay at home orders. Being that these are all sensitive topics, callers may be reluctant to divulge information unprompted. Building trust and a safe environment allows the caller to feel comfortable speaking about their situations and thought processes.

Card Sort

We conducted two card sort studies - one open card sort and a closed card sort. Our goal with these card sort studies was to understand people’s mental models and how they categorize different icons into groups.

Findings

Out of the 25 icons that we had, there were 5 that a majority of our users incorrectly categorized into the buckets for Medical, Report and Mental. These were the icons for Extreme Weather, Housing, Unknown Problem, Welfare Check and Disability.

Survey

We also conducted a qualtrics survey to further understand icon comprehension. We showed our participants pictures of the icons in our app, absent a text descriptor, and asked them to label them with just one word.

Findings

For 5 icons, there was a clear mismatch between how we labeled them and how our participants labeled them. These were the icons for Unconscious, Mental Health, Suicide, Sexual Assualt and Unknown Problem.

Quantitative Research

We analyzed publicly available 911 calls for service data for 6 cities in order to understand existing call center categorization schemes of emergencies. This involved data cleaning, feature engineering, and call type standardization.

Findings

After standardizing hundreds of call types, we found the most common call types revolved around traffic, property crime, assisting the public, suspicious persons, domestic violence, drugs, and behavioral health.
