37 Million Americans can't hear or speak out loud.

Despite this, 70% of 911 call centers in the US are unable to receive text messages. The legacy equipment these call centers use is expensive and difficult to replace, and can’t receive text messages or detect location from mobile devices. This leaves deaf and hard of hearing people with often suboptimal methods for contacting 911 when they are in the throes of an emergency situation.

1 in 10 Fatal police encounters have involved an individual with a known mental illness.

Nationally, 104 people undergoing a mental health crisis were fatally shot by police in 2021 alone. In the majority of the country, police have little training to deal with situations involving people with mental illness.

7-10% of US police contacts involve persons with mental illnesses, with a disproportionate amount of these encounters resulting in arrest

Offenses tend to be minor, such as trespassing or disorderly conduct. An estimated 685,000 people with severe mental illness are incarcerated nationally every year.

An estimated 44% of people in jail and 37% of people in prison have a mental health condition.

And 10 - 25% suffer severe mental health concerns such as schizophrenia. Correctional facilities have become de-facto mental health institutions, yet often don’t provide the treatment people really need.

Accessibility and equity in emergency services is complex. To address the challenge, our team partnered with accesSOS, a nonprofit organization that aims to make emergency services accessible to people unable to call 911, and who do not have text to 911 in their area.

We set out to learn about the emergency services domain, understand how diverse populations interact with the services, and how we can develop a trusted solution that meets their needs.

When people think of emergency services in the United States, they think of 911. Whether there is a fire, medical emergency, or criminal event, people call 911 for assistance. But sometimes, it isn’t possible to verbally interact through a call. Other times, interaction with 911 is avoided because of historical injustices embedded within the system.

All of these individuals have different experiences reaching out to or being the subject of a call to 911. We can’t shape how emergency services act in a situation. But we can design a system that ensures equal access to contacting emergency services, and that captures information that is more likely to influence a more appropriate response. With this mission in mind, we began developing our design for a progressive web app for accesSOS.

The debate around policing and 911 in the United States is active and robust. The issues are numerous, particularly in regards to racial inequities and the militarization of police. As such, we felt it necessary to narrowly focus on specific facets of the problem space.

Our research focused on two primary areas:

  1. User needs for a solution that enables them to contact both traditional emergency services (police, medical, fire) and mental crisis services.
  2. Understanding the current emergency services landscape at both social and infrastructural levels to be able to incorporate an app into the existing system.
